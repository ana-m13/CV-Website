    • OriginALevel

    The project addresses the challenges faced by A level computer science students who may struggle to seek personalized help from busy teachers, leading to potential knowledge gaps and diminished confidence. Recognizing the reluctance of some students to ask questions in class, the project proposes a web application where students can anonymously or openly ask computer science-related questions. While the initial idea included an anonymous function, it was omitted to prevent misuse. The platform allows both students and teachers to contribute answers, fostering a collaborative learning environment. The questions are categorized by an algorithm for easy retrieval, and a rating system facilitates the identification of well-answered queries. To ensure reliability, teacher responses are distinguished with green or pink ticks, requiring a verification code for identity. The login system and student authentication enhance security within the school community. The primary stakeholders are students seeking additional support, aiming to alleviate stress and anxiety by providing a platform for continuous learning and interaction. The project envisions creating a sense of community among computer science students, fostering engagement and potentially improving overall subject performance.

    Add Blocker

    In my second project, I configured a Raspberry Pi to run Pi-hole, an ad-blocking and DNS filtering software. The setup involved installing the operating system on an SD card and resolving SSH key issues by editing the known_hosts file. I configured Pi-hole to use Quad9 for DNS filtering, actively blocking malicious content. To enhance interaction, I utilized the admin web interface and enabled logging to monitor DNS activity on the network. I incorporated community-maintained blocklists, such as "The Unofficial Blocklist for Pi-hole," to block YouTube ads. Additionally, I turned Pi-hole into a DNS server, redirecting all internet traffic through it. Addressing potential questions, I considered DNS security, discussed real-world DNS-based attacks like DNS amplification attacks, and highlighted the protective role of Quad9 as a global DNS resolver against malware and phishing.
